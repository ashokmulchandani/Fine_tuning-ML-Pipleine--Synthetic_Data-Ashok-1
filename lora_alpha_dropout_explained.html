Why Alpha & Dropout Exist in LoRA

Two knobs that prevent the adapter from being too weak, too strong, or too memorized

🎚 lora_alpha — The Volume Knob

Problem: r Limits the Adapter's Influence

With r=8, B×A is a very LOW-RANK matrix — it can only express 8 "patterns" of change. The raw values in B×A might be too small to have real impact. Alpha amplifies them.

The formula: ΔW = (alpha / r) × B × A
With r=8, alpha=16 → scaling = 16/8 = 2.0×

🧠 Why not just use a larger r instead? Because alpha lets you keep r small (few params) while still having impact. r controls the adapter's complexity (how many patterns). Alpha controls its volume (how loud). These are independent. You can have a simple adapter (r=8) that speaks loudly (alpha=16).

🎲 lora_dropout — The Forget-Mechanism

Problem: Small Datasets → Overfitting

If you only have 500 training examples, a 37M-param adapter can easily memorize them instead of learning general patterns. Dropout prevents this.

🧠 How dropout works: Each training step, a DIFFERENT random 10% of LoRA connections are turned off. This forces the adapter to NOT rely on any single connection — it must spread the learning across all of them. Think of it like studying: if you re-read the same highlighted paragraph (no dropout), you memorize it. If someone randomly blacks out parts of your notes each time (dropout), you're forced to understand the concept.

📋 Quick Reference

lora_alpha

Default:16
Range:8–32 typical
Low (<8):Adapter too quiet
High (>32):Risk overwriting base model
Rule:alpha = 2×r is standard

lora_dropout

Default:0.1
Range:0.05–0.2 typical
0.0:No regularization — risk overfitting
0.2+:Too much — adapter struggles to learn
Rule:0.05 for large data, 0.1 for small data
